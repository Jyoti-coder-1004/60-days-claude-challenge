Welcome

Train your eye before you train your judgment.

Every day you scroll past headlines and posts engineered to make you feel something before you've had a chance to think. This isn't a quiz — there's no failing. You'll look at real-feeling examples, form your own gut reaction first, then we'll reveal exactly what's happening underneath the words.

How this works: Two short challenges. In each one, you'll react first — then we reveal the analysis together, one layer at a time.
Why it matters: media literacy isn't about being cynical about everything you read. It's about noticing the specific techniques that turn information into a reaction, so you can choose what to believe on purpose instead of by accident.

Challenge 1 · Headline Detective

The gap between a headline and the truth

Headlines are written to earn a click, not necessarily to summarize a story accurately. Writers often use vague sourcing, absolute language, and manufactured urgency to make something ordinary sound extraordinary.

Why it matters: Studies on news consumption consistently find that a large share of readers share or form opinions from a headline alone, without reading the article underneath. If the headline is exaggerated, the misunderstanding spreads far past the original story.
In everyday life: this is the same instinct that makes you forward a message before checking it, or repeat a "fact" you only half-read. Slowing down at the headline stage is the cheapest media-literacy habit there is.

Here's how it works: you'll read a fictional headline and article, tell us if you'd click it, try to spot the exaggerated parts yourself, and then we'll reveal the full breakdown.

Challenge 2 · Emotion Detector

If it made you feel something fast, ask why

Beyond headlines, social posts and captions are often built around a single emotional trigger — fear, urgency, envy, belonging — because emotional arousal is what drives shares, comments, and watch-time.

Why it matters: Content optimized for engagement isn't necessarily optimized for truth. The two can align, but they don't have to — and creators, advertisers, and algorithms are rewarded for the reaction, not the accuracy.
In everyday life: this is why an angry post about a stranger spreads faster than a calm correction, and why "you won't believe this" captions outperform plain descriptions. Noticing the emotional lever is the first step to not being pulled by it.

You'll read a short post, name the feeling it produced in you and the words responsible, then we'll reveal the technique behind it.
